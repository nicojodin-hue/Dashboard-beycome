How to Sell Your Florida Home Without a Realtor in 2026

Updated April 21, 2026 · 12 min read

Florida's real estate market remains one of the most active in the country, with the average home price hovering around $415,000 as of early 2026. A strong influx of out-of-state buyers, a zero state income tax environment, and year-round demand make the Sunshine State a seller's market in most metro areas. For homeowners willing to handle the process themselves, selling FSBO in Florida can mean keeping an extra $12,000–$25,000 in your pocket rather than paying a 3% listing agent commission.

Why FSBO Works in Florida

Florida is one of the friendlier states for for-sale-by-owner transactions. Here's why sellers succeed here:

  • No attorney requirement at closing. Unlike Georgia or North Carolina, Florida does not require a real estate attorney to be present at closing. A licensed title company can handle the entire transaction.
  • Estimated average savings of $12,450. On a $415,000 home, skipping the listing agent's 3% commission saves approximately $12,450 before closing costs.
  • About 7% of Florida home sales are FSBO. That's tens of thousands of transactions per year, meaning buyers and their agents are comfortable with the process.
  • Flat Fee MLS access. Services like Beycome allow Florida FSBO sellers to list on the MLS for as little as $99, putting your home in front of every buyer's agent in your market.
  • Fast markets in key metros. Orlando, Tampa, and Jacksonville consistently see median days-on-market under 30 days, meaning a well-priced home sells quickly with or without a listing agent.

Florida FSBO Laws & Required Disclosures

Florida law requires sellers to disclose all known material defects that would affect the value or desirability of the property. Failure to disclose can expose you to lawsuits after closing. The following table summarizes the key required disclosures:

Disclosure Required? Notes
Seller's Property Disclosure (FS 689.261) Yes — legally required Must disclose all known material defects including roof, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and structural issues
Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Yes — federal law (homes built before 1978) Must provide EPA pamphlet and give buyer 10-day inspection period
HOA / Condo Association Disclosure Yes — if applicable Must disclose HOA fees, rules, and pending special assessments; buyer has 3-day right of rescission
Radon Gas Disclosure Yes — Florida Statute 404.056 Standard one-paragraph notice about radon gas in Florida soil must be included in purchase contract
Flood Zone Disclosure Yes — strongly recommended Disclose if property is in a FEMA flood zone; failure to disclose can void the sale
Energy Efficiency Rating Yes — FS 553.996 Must offer buyer an energy efficiency rating brochure

Florida Closing Costs for Sellers

Florida closing costs for sellers typically run between 1.5% and 3% of the sale price (excluding agent commission). Understanding these costs helps you net more from your FSBO sale.

Cost Item Who Pays Typical Amount
Documentary Stamp Tax on Deed Seller $0.70 per $100 of sale price (~$2,905 on $415K)
Title Insurance (Owner's Policy) Seller (South FL) / Negotiable (North FL) ~$2,000–$3,500 depending on sale price and county
Title Search & Exam Seller $150–$350
Recording Fees Buyer (typically) $10–$50 per document
Documentary Stamp Tax on Mortgage Buyer $0.35 per $100 of mortgage amount
Prorated Property Taxes Seller (Jan 1 to closing date) Varies — based on your tax bill
HOA Transfer Fee (if applicable) Negotiable $100–$500

Miami-Dade County note: Miami-Dade uses a different documentary stamp structure — $0.60 per $100 on the deed plus a $0.45 per $100 surtax, making it slightly higher than other Florida counties.

Step-by-Step FSBO Process in Florida

  1. Price your home accurately. Use a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA), Beycome's free property estimator, or a licensed appraiser. Overpricing is the #1 reason FSBO homes stall.
  2. Prepare and stage your home. Declutter, deep clean, and make any deferred repairs. Professional photos are worth every penny — homes with professional photography sell 32% faster.
  3. List on the Florida MLS. Use a flat fee MLS service like Beycome ($99) to get your home on Zillow, Realtor.com, and every buyer's agent search system in your area.
  4. Complete required disclosures. Fill out the Florida Seller's Disclosure form and prepare your lead paint, radon, and HOA disclosures before the first showing.
  5. Schedule and manage showings. Use a lockbox and an online showing scheduler. Be flexible — weekends and evenings are peak times.
  6. Review and negotiate offers. Read every term carefully: price, financing contingency, inspection period (typically 10–15 days in FL), and closing date. Counter-offers are normal and expected.
  7. Open escrow with a title company. Once you have an accepted offer, choose a reputable title company or real estate attorney. They'll coordinate the title search, handle escrow deposits, and prepare the closing documents.
  8. Navigate the inspection period. The buyer will likely hire a home inspector. Be prepared to negotiate repairs or offer a credit — it's very common in Florida.
  9. Close the transaction. Sign the closing documents, transfer the deed, and collect your proceeds. In Florida, closings are typically handled by the title company at a closing office.

Florida Property Tax Overview

Understanding Florida's property tax system is critical for both buyers and sellers. Florida uses a folio number (also called a parcel ID or property ID) as the unique identifier for each property in the county's tax records. The folio number is used by county property appraisers to track ownership, assessed value, exemptions, and tax bills.

Florida's Homestead Exemption provides up to $50,000 off the assessed value for primary residences, with the first $25,000 applying to all tax levies and the second $25,000 applying to non-school levies. The Save Our Homes (SOH) cap limits annual increases in assessed value to 3% per year or the CPI, whichever is lower — a significant benefit in rapidly appreciating markets like Miami and Orlando.

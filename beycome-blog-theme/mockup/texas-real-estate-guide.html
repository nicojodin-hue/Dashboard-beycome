How to Sell Your Texas Home Without a Realtor in 2026

Updated April 21, 2026 · 12 min read

Texas has one of the most active real estate markets in the United States, driven by strong job growth in Houston, Dallas, Austin, and San Antonio, as well as no state income tax. The average Texas home price sits around $305,000 as of early 2026, and about 8% of all Texas home sales are completed FSBO — one of the highest rates in the country. Texas also offers one major financial advantage most states don't: there is no real estate transfer tax in Texas, making it even cheaper to close a sale as a FSBO seller.

Why FSBO Works in Texas

Texas sellers who go FSBO enjoy several distinct advantages compared to sellers in other states:

  • No state transfer tax. Texas is one of only a handful of states nationwide with zero real estate transfer tax. On a $305,000 sale, that can save $600–$3,000 compared to states like Florida, California, or North Carolina.
  • Estimated average savings of $9,150. On a $305,000 home, eliminating the listing agent's 3% commission saves approximately $9,150.
  • 8% FSBO rate — highest among large states. Texas buyers and buyer's agents are fully accustomed to working with FSBO sellers, making the negotiation process smooth.
  • TREC-regulated contracts. Texas has standardized purchase contracts regulated by the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC), making it straightforward for FSBO sellers to use without needing to draft a custom agreement.
  • Strong market fundamentals. Austin, Dallas, and Houston consistently rank among the fastest-moving markets in the US. Well-priced homes routinely go under contract within 14–21 days.

Texas FSBO Laws & Required Disclosures

Texas has clear statutory disclosure requirements for residential sellers. The TREC Seller's Disclosure Notice (Form OP-H) is required by law under Texas Property Code Section 5.008 for most residential property sales. Here's the full disclosure breakdown:

Disclosure Required? Notes
TREC Seller's Disclosure Notice (OP-H) Yes — Texas Property Code §5.008 Must be delivered before or at contract execution; covers roof, foundation, plumbing, HVAC, appliances, and known hazards
Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Yes — federal law (homes built before 1978) Must provide EPA pamphlet; buyer gets 10-day inspection window
HOA Addendum Yes — if applicable TREC-standardized addendum; must disclose HOA fees, dues, and restrictions; buyer has 3-day right to terminate
MUD (Municipal Utility District) Notice Yes — if in MUD Required for properties within a MUD; discloses tax rate and debt service
Notice of Obligation to Pay Improvement District Assessment Yes — if applicable Required if property is subject to a public improvement district (PID) assessment

Texas Closing Costs for Sellers

One of the most FSBO-friendly aspects of Texas real estate is the absence of a transfer tax. Texas seller closing costs typically run 1–2% of the sale price (excluding agent commission), which is among the lowest in the country.

Cost Item Who Pays Typical Amount
State Transfer Tax N/A $0 — Texas has no transfer tax
Title Insurance (Owner's Policy) Seller (by custom) ~$1,800–$2,800 on $305K home (promulgated rates)
Title Insurance (Lender's Policy) Buyer ~$200–$500 (simultaneous issue discount)
Escrow / Closing Fee Split or seller $300–$600
Survey Buyer (typically) $400–$700 (often required by lender)
Recording Fees Buyer $50–$150
Prorated Property Taxes Seller (Jan 1 to closing) Varies — Texas property taxes are among the highest in the nation, averaging 1.6–1.8% of value annually

Important note on title insurance: In Texas, title insurance rates are set (promulgated) by the state — unlike most states where rates are negotiated. This means the cost is the same regardless of which title company you choose, so focus on service quality rather than price shopping.

Step-by-Step FSBO Process in Texas

  1. Price your Texas home. Use your county's Central Appraisal District (CAD) data, Beycome's free estimator, or a licensed appraiser. Texas CAD valuations are publicly available and a useful starting point, though market value often differs significantly.
  2. Prepare your disclosures. Download the TREC Seller's Disclosure Notice (OP-H) from trec.texas.gov and complete it thoroughly before your first showing. Prepare HOA and MUD addendums if applicable.
  3. List on the Texas MLS. Use a flat fee MLS service like Beycome to get listed on the Multiple Listing Service for $99. Your listing will auto-populate to Zillow, Realtor.com, and every buyer agent's search dashboard.
  4. Market your home. Yard sign, professional photos, virtual tour, and social media posts all help. Texas buyers are active on social media — Facebook Marketplace and Nextdoor are particularly effective for local reach.
  5. Receive and review offers. Buyers will typically use the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract. Review the option period (typically 7–10 days in Texas — this is the standard inspection/due diligence window), earnest money amount, financing contingency, and closing date.
  6. Open title with a Texas title company. Once you have an executed contract, the title company opens escrow, orders a title search, and schedules the closing. Texas closings are handled exclusively by title companies (not attorneys).
  7. Navigate the option period and inspection. The buyer pays an "option fee" (typically $200–$500) for the right to terminate during the option period for any reason. After the option period, the contract is typically binding with limited outs.
  8. Close at the title company. Attend closing, sign documents, and receive your net proceeds via wire transfer or certified check, typically the same day.

Texas Property Tax Overview

Texas has no state income tax, but partially compensates with property taxes that rank among the highest in the nation — averaging 1.6% to 1.8% of property value annually. Each county's Central Appraisal District (CAD) assigns a unique property ID to each parcel, which is used to track ownership, assessed value, exemptions, and tax bills.

Texas homeowners benefit from a $100,000 homestead exemption from school district taxes (effective 2023 via HB 2) and optional local exemptions offered by cities and counties. The annual protest deadline is May 15 (or 30 days after your Notice of Appraised Value, whichever is later). Given that Texas CAD valuations sometimes exceed market value — especially in fast-moving markets like Austin — protesting your appraisal is worth doing every year.

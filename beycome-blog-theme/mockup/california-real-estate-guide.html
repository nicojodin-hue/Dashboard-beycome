How to Sell Your California Home Without a Realtor in 2026

Updated April 21, 2026 · 13 min read

California has the most complex real estate disclosure requirements of any US state and the highest average home prices — approximately $785,000 statewide as of early 2026. That high price point also means the financial reward for FSBO is unmatched: avoiding a 3% listing agent commission on a $785,000 California home saves you roughly $23,550. On a $1.2M property in Los Angeles or the Bay Area, the savings exceed $36,000. About 5% of California home sales are FSBO — lower than other states due to the complexity, but very rewarding for prepared sellers.

Why FSBO Works in California

  • Highest potential savings in the country. The average $785,000 California home generates $23,550 in savings at 3% commission. Bay Area and LA luxury properties can save sellers $40,000–$60,000 or more.
  • No attorney requirement at closing. Despite California's complex disclosures, the actual closing is handled by a title company — no real estate attorney is required, keeping closing costs manageable.
  • Strong seller's markets in key metros. While prices have normalized since the 2022 peak, demand remains high in San Diego, Orange County, and the Bay Area. Multiple-offer situations are still common on well-priced properties.
  • Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) standardization. California's required TDS is a well-known, standardized form that sophisticated buyers and their agents fully understand — reducing friction in FSBO transactions.
  • MLS access via flat fee brokers. Beycome and similar services provide California FSBO sellers with full MLS access for $99, reaching every buyer's agent in your county and statewide.

California FSBO Laws & Required Disclosures

California has the most extensive disclosure requirements of any US state. Sellers must provide a comprehensive package of disclosures before the buyer removes contingencies. Missing a required disclosure can give the buyer the right to rescind the contract and recover damages.

Disclosure Required? Notes
Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) Yes — Civil Code §1102 The foundational CA disclosure; covers seller's knowledge of property condition, defects, neighborhood nuisances, and known neighborhood issues. Buyer has 3-day right to rescind after receipt.
Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD) Yes — Civil Code §1103 Prepared by a third-party NHD company; discloses whether property is in a flood zone, fire hazard zone, earthquake fault zone, seismic hazard zone, or wildland fire area. Cost: ~$100–$130.
Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Yes — federal law (pre-1978 homes) Must provide EPA pamphlet; buyer gets 10-day inspection right
Mello-Roos Disclosure Yes — if property is in a CFD Must disclose Community Facilities District (Mello-Roos) special tax obligations; buyer has 3-day rescission right after receipt of Notice of Special Tax
HOA Disclosure Package Yes — if applicable (Civil Code §4525) Must provide CC&Rs, bylaws, rules, financial statements, meeting minutes, and pending litigation; comprehensive package required within 10 days of request
Smoke Detector / Water Heater Compliance Yes — Health & Safety Code Seller must certify that smoke detectors are installed per state code and water heater is strapped for earthquake safety
Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure (AVID) N/A for FSBO (agent-only form) FSBO sellers are not required to complete this agent-specific form, but should ensure the TDS is thorough

California Closing Costs for Sellers

California has one of the more complex closing cost structures in the US, with a statewide documentary transfer tax plus additional city-level transfer taxes in some jurisdictions (notably Los Angeles, Oakland, and San Francisco).

Cost Item Who Pays Typical Amount
County Documentary Transfer Tax Seller (by custom) $1.10 per $1,000 of sale price (~$863 on $785K home)
City Transfer Tax (if applicable) Varies by city Los Angeles: $4.50/$1,000 extra; Oakland: $15/$1,000 (over $300K); SF: up to $15/$1,000 on sales over $10M
Title Insurance (Owner's Policy) Seller (by custom in Southern CA) / Buyer (Northern CA) ~$2,500–$5,000 on $785K home (varies by county custom)
Escrow Fee Split evenly (by custom) $2.00 per $1,000 of sale price + $250 base (~$1,820 each on $785K)
Natural Hazard Disclosure Report Seller $100–$130 (third-party NHD company)
Home Warranty (optional) Seller (by custom) $400–$700 (one-year buyer's home warranty)
Prorated Property Taxes Seller (July 1 fiscal year to closing) Varies — CA property taxes due in November and March each year

Supplemental tax bills: After a California real estate sale, the county assessor reassesses the property at its new sale price and issues one or two supplemental tax bills. These arrive after closing and are typically the buyer's responsibility, but confirm this in your purchase contract.

Step-by-Step FSBO Process in California

  1. Price your California home. California's diverse markets mean a $785K average masks enormous variation — from $450K in Fresno to $1.8M in the Bay Area. Research your specific neighborhood using Zillow, Redfin, or public county assessor records (searchable by APN). Consider a licensed appraiser for accurate valuation on unique properties.
  2. Prepare the full disclosure package. Order the Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD) report early ($100–$130 from a third-party provider). Complete the Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) thoroughly. If in a HOA, gather the full HOA document package. If in a Mello-Roos CFD, obtain the Notice of Special Tax from the tax administrator.
  3. Hire a reputable escrow company. In California, real estate closings are handled by licensed escrow companies (or title companies with escrow authority). Select your escrow early and give them your preliminary title and disclosure materials.
  4. List on the California MLS. Use a flat fee MLS service like Beycome ($99) to reach all active buyer's agents in your county. California's multiple regional MLSs (CRMLS, SFAR, BAREIS, SDMLS) all connect through cooperating systems.
  5. Stage and photograph professionally. California buyers — especially in the $700K+ range — expect immaculate presentation. Professional staging and photography are standard, not optional, in competitive CA markets.
  6. Review offers carefully. California purchase contracts (CAR Form RPA) are detailed. Pay attention to the contingency removal timeline (typically 17 days for financing and appraisal, 10 days for inspection), loan type, down payment percentage, and close of escrow date.
  7. Open escrow and deliver disclosures. Deliver your full disclosure package within 3–7 days of contract execution. The buyer has 3 days to rescind after receiving the TDS and NHD.
  8. Navigate contingency removal. After the inspection and appraisal periods, buyer removes contingencies in writing. Once all contingencies are removed, the deal is very close to binding.
  9. Close escrow. Sign closing documents at the escrow office or via mobile notary. Funds typically wire within 1–2 business days of all documents being signed and recorded.

California Property Tax Overview

California uses an APN (Assessor's Parcel Number) as the unique property tax identifier assigned by each county assessor. Every parcel in California has a unique APN, used to track ownership, the Proposition 13 base year assessed value, exemptions, and all tax bills (regular, supplemental, and escaped assessments).

Proposition 13 (1978) caps annual increases in assessed value at 2% per year — until the property sells. Upon sale, the property is reassessed at the new purchase price, which often creates a significant jump in property tax for the buyer. The base property tax rate is 1% of assessed value plus voter-approved bonds and Mello-Roos special taxes, which can add 0.2%–2% depending on location.

For a complete guide to finding your California APN, understanding your Prop 13 base year value, reading your supplemental tax bill, and filing for homeowner's exemption, read: California Property Tax ID: How to Find Your APN.

How to Sell Your Georgia Home Without a Realtor in 2026

Updated April 21, 2026 · 11 min read

Georgia's real estate market has been one of the strongest in the Southeast for the past several years, with Atlanta consistently ranking among the top US cities for population growth and job creation. The average Georgia home price sits around $318,000 as of early 2026, and approximately 6% of all residential sales in the state are completed FSBO. While Georgia requires a real estate attorney at closing — unlike Florida or Texas — the rest of the FSBO process is straightforward, and the potential savings are substantial.

Why FSBO Works in Georgia

Georgia has several characteristics that make FSBO a viable and rewarding path for sellers:

  • No seller-paid transfer tax. In Georgia, the intangibles tax of $1.50 per $500 of the loan amount is paid by the buyer, not the seller. Sellers have very low transfer-related costs at closing.
  • Estimated average savings of $9,540. On a $318,000 home, avoiding the 3% listing agent commission saves approximately $9,540.
  • Standardized disclosure form widely available. The Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) Seller's Property Disclosure form is the industry-standard document, and FSBO sellers can use an equivalent disclosure without being members of the GAR.
  • Strong metro Atlanta demand. Tech, logistics, and film industry growth keep demand high in Atlanta and its suburbs — Marietta, Roswell, Sandy Springs, and Alpharetta consistently see homes sell within 2–3 weeks when priced correctly.
  • Flat Fee MLS access. Services like Beycome provide Georgia FSBO sellers with full MLS exposure for $99, reaching every buyer's agent in the state.

Georgia FSBO Laws & Required Disclosures

Georgia does not have a statutory mandate requiring sellers to complete a specific disclosure form (unlike Texas), but the common law duty to disclose all known material defects applies. In practice, using the standard Seller's Property Disclosure Statement is strongly recommended to protect against post-closing liability.

Disclosure Required? Notes
Seller's Property Disclosure Statement Not legally mandated — but strongly advised Standard practice; covers roof, foundation, plumbing, HVAC, pest damage, and environmental hazards. Protects seller from future litigation.
Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Yes — federal law (homes built before 1978) Must provide EPA pamphlet; buyer gets 10-day inspection right
HOA Disclosure Yes — if applicable (O.C.G.A. §44-3-111) Must provide HOA documents within 10 days of request; buyer has 3-day right of rescission after receipt
Property Tax Assessment Notice Recommended Disclose the most recent property tax notice and any pending reassessments or appeals
Known Material Defects Yes — common law duty Georgia common law requires disclosure of all known latent defects not visible to a reasonable inspector

Georgia Closing Costs for Sellers

Georgia seller closing costs are among the lower in the country, thanks to the absence of a seller-paid transfer tax. The biggest variable for sellers is typically the real estate attorney's closing fee and title-related costs.

Cost Item Who Pays Typical Amount
Intangibles Tax (on mortgage) Buyer $1.50 per $500 of mortgage amount (buyer's cost only)
Real Estate Attorney Fee Buyer typically — sometimes split $500–$900 (required at every Georgia closing)

Title Insurance (Owner's Policy) Buyer (by custom in Georgia) ~$800–$1,800 depending on sale price

Recording Fees Buyer $25–$100
Prorated Property Taxes Seller (Jan 1 to closing) Varies — Georgia property taxes due in October/December

Attorney closing requirement: Georgia is one of the "attorney close" states. A licensed Georgia real estate attorney must examine title and oversee the disbursement of funds at closing. When selling FSBO, the buyer's attorney typically handles closing — be sure to confirm this arrangement early in the transaction.

Step-by-Step FSBO Process in Georgia

  1. Price your home accurately. Pull recent comparable sales from your county's tax records (publicly searchable by Map-Block-Parcel number) or use Beycome's free estimator. The Atlanta metro is a sophisticated market — pricing within 2–3% of true market value is critical.
  2. Complete your disclosures. Use the standard Seller's Property Disclosure Statement. Even though it's not legally required, filling it out completely protects you from post-closing claims and makes buyers more confident.
  3. List on the Georgia MLS. Use a flat fee MLS service like Beycome for $99 to reach every buyer's agent in Atlanta, Savannah, Augusta, and beyond.
  4. Show your home. Georgia buyers move fast in competitive markets like Buckhead, Smyrna, and Decatur. Use a lockbox and be prepared for multiple offers on well-priced homes.
  5. Review and negotiate offers. Georgia purchase contracts typically include a due diligence period (10–15 days) and a financing contingency. Pay attention to the earnest money amount and the closing date.
  6. Select a closing attorney. In Georgia, the buyer's attorney handles the closing, but as the seller you should confirm their credentials and timeline. Some FSBO sellers retain their own attorney to review the closing documents independently.
  7. Navigate the due diligence period. The buyer will conduct a home inspection. In Georgia, buyers can terminate for any reason during due diligence — be prepared to negotiate repairs or price adjustments.
  8. Close with the attorney. Attend the closing, execute the warranty deed, and receive your net proceeds. Georgia closings typically happen at the closing attorney's office.

Georgia Property Tax Overview

Georgia uses a Map-Block-Parcel (MBP) number system as the primary property identification number in most counties. This unique identifier — assigned by the county tax assessor — is used to look up ownership records, assessed value, exemption status, and tax bills in county property databases.

Georgia's homestead exemption provides a $2,000 reduction in the assessed value for qualifying primary residences statewide, with many counties and cities offering additional local exemptions. The assessment ratio in Georgia is 40% of fair market value, meaning a $318,000 home is assessed at approximately $127,200 for tax purposes. Property tax returns are due by April 1 each year if you're filing for exemptions or reporting changes in ownership.
